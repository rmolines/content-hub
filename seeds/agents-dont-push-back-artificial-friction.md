## Raw context

From thesis.md — in human teams, push-back happens naturally: a developer questions a vague spec, a reviewer challenges an approach, a PM asks "do we really need this?" Agents don't do any of that — they execute what's written. The feature-lifecycle process creates structural friction: /discovery questions vague assumptions, /review validates against PRD, /planning refuses scope that won't decompose cleanly. Each phase acts as an artificial check that replaces the organic resistance humans provide.

## Why this matters

Challenges the "AI makes everything frictionless" narrative. The article would argue that some friction is load-bearing — it catches bad ideas, prevents scope creep, and forces clarity. When you remove humans from the loop, you don't remove the need for friction; you need to design it into the process explicitly. Frictionless execution of bad plans is worse than slow execution of good ones.
